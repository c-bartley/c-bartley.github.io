Chris Bartley

PhD researcher at the University of Sheffield, building speech and language technology to help preserve endangered languages — starting with Manx Gaelic.

Research interests

My doctoral research at the University of Sheffield focuses on making modern speech and language technology accessible to endangered and low-resource languages. I work primarily with Manx Gaelic (Gaelg), a Celtic language from the Isle of Man, but the methods I develop are designed to be language-agnostic and transferable.

Low-resource speech technology — Building ASR, TTS, and voice conversion systems that bootstrap from very small corpora — tens of minutes to a few hours of audio — using semi-supervised learning, data augmentation, and cross-lingual transfer from related languages.

Endangered language technology — Bringing these methods to endangered and minority languages, primarily Manx Gaelic, and building practical tools for revitalisation such as speech synthesis, recognition, and translation. The approaches are language-agnostic and transfer across the Celtic family and beyond.

Domain adaptation — Closing the gap between the clean, read speech that models are trained on and the spontaneous speech that dominates real recordings. I use voice conversion (Grad-VC / DiffVC) as offline data augmentation, training on cross-speaker, content-matched word and phone pairs rather than same-voice reconstruction. Listen to sample training pairs.

Unsupervised learning — Speech models that learn from audio without transcriptions, with a focus on graphemic approaches — including a graphemic formulation of wav2vec-U 2.0 that maps speech directly to characters rather than phonemes, removing the dependence on a phonemic lexicon.
